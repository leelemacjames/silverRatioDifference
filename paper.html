Working Paper · Algebraic Number Theory

Metallic Means and Differences of Consecutive Square Roots

Lee McCulloch-James
Draft, 2026
Abstract

For the silver ratio, every positive integer power of its reciprocal can be written as a difference $\sqrt{N+1} - \sqrt{N}$ of square roots of consecutive integers. We generalise this question to the full metallic-mean family (parameter $k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$) and find a sharp parity phenomenon: for even $k$ the representation holds for every exponent, while for odd $k$ it holds exactly for exponents divisible by 3. Explicit generators and recurrences are given. The period-3 pattern for odd $k$ is traced to the cyclotomic polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$, the minimal polynomial of primitive cube roots of unity, acting in the mod-4 quotient of a Lucas-type recurrence. The even/odd dichotomy reflects the structure of rings of integers in quadratic fields: when $k$ is odd, the ring $\mathbb{Z}[(1+\sqrt{D})/2]$ has a half-integer lattice that forces the period-3 oscillation.

Notation and conventions
$k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$: the metallic-mean parameter
$\delta_k = (k + \sqrt{k^2 + 4})/2$: the metallic mean (positive root of $x^2 - kx - 1 = 0$)
$\delta_k^{-1}$: its reciprocal, lying in $(0,1)$; the quantity whose powers we study
$S_n(k) = \delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n}$: a Lucas-type trace sequence
$M_n(k) = (S_n - 2)/4$: the generator (candidate integer under the square root)
$\lambda_k = \delta_k^2$: the squared metallic mean
$D = k^2 + 4$: the discriminant of the characteristic quadratic
$\mathcal{O}_K$: the ring of integers of $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{D})$

1Preamble: from a Twitter observation to a generating function

In October 2022, the account @MathsTechnology posted the following three identities:

$$(\sqrt{2} - 1)^2 = \sqrt{9} - \sqrt{8},$$
$$(\sqrt{2} - 1)^3 = \sqrt{50} - \sqrt{49},$$
$$(\sqrt{2} - 1)^4 = \sqrt{289} - \sqrt{288},$$

and asked whether every positive integer power of $\sqrt{2} - 1$ is the difference of square roots of consecutive integers. The integers under the roots grow rapidly: $8, 9$; then $49, 50$; then $288, 289$. Where do they come from?

The first instinct is empirical. Expand $(\sqrt{2}-1)^n$ using the binomial theorem. Each power produces a compound surd of the form $a + m\sqrt{2}$, where $a$ and $m$ are integers (the integer part $a$ appears, on inspection, to be prime for the first several cases). Read off the integers under the square roots. Call the smaller one $P_n$. The first few values are:

$$\{P_n\} = \{0, 1, 8, 49, 288, 1681, 9800, 57121, 332928, \ldots\}$$

This is OEIS A001108, whose recursive formula is $P_{n+1} = 6P_n - P_{n-1} + 2$ with $P_0 = 0$ and $P_1 = 1$. But where does the recurrence come from? And does it work for all $n$?

The brute-force route, developed in a McMurmerings blog post and an accompanying spreadsheet, proceeds by expanding each power, reconstructing the compound surds, spotting the interleaving pattern of $a_n$ and $m_n$, and finally arriving at an explicit generator:

$$P_n = \frac{1 - a^{2n} - 2a^n}{4a^n}, \qquad a \equiv (\sqrt{2}-1)^2 = 3 - 2\sqrt{2}.$$ (0)

This works. But it is specific to $k = 2$ (the silver ratio), it was found by pattern-matching rather than derivation, and it gives no hint as to which other metallic means might share the property. The present paper provides the cleaner algebraic route. The key insight is to solve the inverse problem first: given $y$, when can $y = \sqrt{M+1} - \sqrt{M}$ for integer $M$? This produces the generator formula in one step, the Lucas-type recurrence follows immediately, and the generalisation to all metallic means reveals a sharp even/odd dichotomy that the blog-post approach could not see.

Pedagogical note
The progression from idle observation through spreadsheet experiment to clean algebraic derivation is the intended reading order of the interactive toggle learner that accompanies this paper. The toggle structure mirrors the six stages: observation, sequence extraction, inverse problem, generator, recurrence, and theorem.

2The metallic means

Fix an integer $k \geq 1$ and let $\delta_k$ be the metallic mean, the positive root of

$$x^2 - kx - 1 = 0,$$ (1)

so that

$$\delta_k = \frac{k + \sqrt{k^2 + 4}}{2}.$$ (2)

Then $\delta_k > 1$ and its reciprocal $0 < \delta_k^{-1} < 1$ is the quantity whose powers we study. The conjugate root of equation (1) is $\bar{\delta}_k = (k - \sqrt{k^2+4})/2 = -\delta_k^{-1}$, so that $\delta_k \bar{\delta}_k = -1$.

For $k = 1$: the golden ratio $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2 \approx 1.618$. For $k = 2$: the silver ratio $\delta_2 = 1 + \sqrt{2} \approx 2.414$, whose reciprocal is $\sqrt{2} - 1$. For $k = 3$: the bronze ratio $\delta_3 = (3+\sqrt{13})/2 \approx 3.303$.

3The basic algebraic mechanism

A real number $y \in (0,1)$ can be written in the form

$$y = \sqrt{M+1} - \sqrt{M} \qquad (M \geq 0)$$ (3)

if and only if

Generator formula
$$M = \frac{y^{-2} + y^2 - 2}{4}.$$ (4)

Derivation. From (3), rationalise: $1/y = \sqrt{M+1} + \sqrt{M}$. Adding and subtracting:

$$y + \frac{1}{y} = 2\sqrt{M+1}, \qquad \frac{1}{y} - y = 2\sqrt{M}.$$

Squaring the second equation gives $1/y^2 + y^2 - 2 = 4M$, which is (4). Conversely, if $M$ is defined by (4), then $\sqrt{M+1} - \sqrt{M} = y$ (taking the positive root since $y > 0$).

Therefore, for any fixed $k$ and exponent $n \geq 1$, the only possible candidate is obtained by substituting $y = \delta_k^{-n}$ into (4):

$$M_n(k) := \frac{\delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n} - 2}{4}.$$ (5)

The original question becomes: when is $M_n(k) \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$?

4An integer sequence in the background

Define

$$S_n(k) := \delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n}.$$ (6)

Then $M_n(k) = (S_n - 2)/4$. Let $\lambda_k := \delta_k^2$. Since $\delta_k^2 = k\delta_k + 1$, one checks

$$\lambda_k + \lambda_k^{-1} = \delta_k^2 + \delta_k^{-2} = k^2 + 2.$$

Hence $(S_n)$ is a Lucas-type sequence:

Lucas-type recurrence
$$S_{n+1} = (k^2 + 2)\,S_n - S_{n-1}, \qquad S_0 = 2,\; S_1 = k^2 + 2.$$ (7)

Since the recurrence coefficient and initial values are integers, $S_n \in \mathbb{Z}$ for all $n \geq 0$. The integrality of $M_n(k)$ is therefore purely a congruence question:

$$M_n(k) \in \mathbb{Z} \iff S_n(k) \equiv 2 \pmod{4}.$$ (8)

5Main theorem: even $k$ vs odd $k$

Theorem 1

Let $k \geq 1$ be an integer and let $\delta_k = (k + \sqrt{k^2+4})/2$. Define $M_n(k)$ by (5). Then:

1. If $k$ is even, then $M_n(k) \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ for every $n \geq 1$, and therefore

$$\delta_k^{-n} = \sqrt{M_n(k) + 1} - \sqrt{M_n(k)} \qquad \text{for all } n \geq 1.$$

2. If $k$ is odd, then $M_n(k) \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ if and only if $3 \mid n$, and therefore

$$\delta_k^{-n} = \sqrt{M_n(k) + 1} - \sqrt{M_n(k)} \iff 3 \mid n.$$

Proof. Work modulo 4 in the recurrence (7). Write $A := k^2 + 2$.

Case 1: $k$ even. Then $k^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{4}$, so $A \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ and $S_{n+1} \equiv 2S_n - S_{n-1} \pmod{4}$. Since $S_0 = 2$ and $S_1 = A \equiv 2$, by induction $S_n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ for all $n$. Hence $(S_n - 2)/4$ is always an integer.

Case 2: $k$ odd. Then $k^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, so $A \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ and $S_{n+1} \equiv 3S_n - S_{n-1} \equiv -S_n - S_{n-1} \pmod{4}$. Now $S_0 \equiv 2$ and $S_1 \equiv 3$, so the first terms mod 4 are

$$S_n \equiv 2,\; 3,\; 3,\; 2,\; 3,\; 3,\; 2,\; 3,\; 3,\; \ldots \pmod{4},$$

and the recurrence forces this period-3 pattern to persist. Therefore $S_n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ exactly when $n \equiv 0 \pmod{3}$, i.e. $3 \mid n$. $\square$

Corollary
Whenever $M_n(k)$ is an integer, we have the explicit identity $\delta_k^{-n} = \sqrt{M_n(k) + 1} - \sqrt{M_n(k)}$, with $M_n(k)$ given by (5).

6Examples: golden, silver, bronze

6.1Golden ($k = 1$)

Here $\delta_1 = \varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$. Theorem 1 says the representation holds exactly for $3 \mid n$. Indeed:

$$\varphi^{-3} = \sqrt{5} - \sqrt{4}, \quad \text{since } M_3(1) = 4.$$

Next, $M_6(1) = 80$ and $\varphi^{-6} = \sqrt{81} - \sqrt{80}$.

6.2Silver ($k = 2$)

Here $\delta_2 = 1 + \sqrt{2}$ and the representation holds for all $n$. The first values are

$$M_1(2) = 1,\; M_2(2) = 8,\; M_3(2) = 49,\; M_4(2) = 288, \ldots$$

giving the identities from the Twitter post:

$$(\sqrt{2}-1)^2 = \sqrt{9} - \sqrt{8}, \quad (\sqrt{2}-1)^3 = \sqrt{50} - \sqrt{49}, \quad (\sqrt{2}-1)^4 = \sqrt{289} - \sqrt{288}, \ldots$$

This recovers OEIS A001108 and the generator (0) from the blog post, now derived rather than guessed.

6.3Bronze ($k = 3$)

Here $\delta_3 = (3+\sqrt{13})/2$, and again Theorem 1 says the representation holds exactly for $3 \mid n$. The first working exponent is $n = 3$:

$$M_3(3) = 324, \quad \delta_3^{-3} = \sqrt{325} - \sqrt{324}.$$

Then $n = 6$ gives $M_6(3) = 421200$ and $\delta_3^{-6} = \sqrt{421201} - \sqrt{421200}$.

7A compact recurrence for the generator

When $M_n(k)$ is integral, it inherits a recurrence from (7). Since $S_n = 4M_n + 2$:

$$4M_{n+1} + 2 = (k^2+2)(4M_n + 2) - (4M_{n-1} + 2),$$

which simplifies to

Generator recurrence
$$M_{n+1} = (k^2+2)\,M_n - M_{n-1} + \frac{k^2}{2}.$$ (9)

This is an integer recurrence precisely when $k$ is even (since $k^2/2 \in \mathbb{Z}$). For odd $k$, one should restrict to the subsequence $n = 3m$, which then satisfies an integer recurrence derived from composing three steps of (7).

For $k = 2$: $M_{n+1} = 6M_n - M_{n-1} + 2$, matching OEIS A001108 exactly.

8Geometric perspectives: why periodicity appears

Theorem 1 was proved by a congruence calculation. We now explain why such a congruence argument must lead to periodic behaviour, and why period 3 is the one that appears when $k$ is odd.

8.1A finite-state viewpoint (why cycles occur)

Reducing the recurrence (7) modulo 4 turns it into an update rule on the finite set $(\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z})^2$:

$$(S_{n+1}, S_n) \equiv (A\,S_n - S_{n-1},\; S_n) \pmod{4}, \qquad A = k^2 + 2.$$

The sequence of pairs $(S_n, S_{n-1}) \bmod 4$ must eventually repeat, and therefore is purely periodic from the start (since $S_0, S_1$ are fixed).

When $k$ is even, $A \equiv 2$ and the orbit is constant at $S_n \equiv 2$.

When $k$ is odd, $A \equiv 3$ and the orbit has period 3, giving the pattern $2, 3, 3, 2, 3, 3, \ldots$

9The cyclotomic polynomial and period 3

The recurrence (7) has characteristic polynomial

$$x^2 - (k^2 + 2)x + 1.$$

Modulo 4, this becomes:

$$\begin{cases} (x-1)^2, & k \text{ even},\\ x^2 + x + 1, & k \text{ odd}. \end{cases}$$

Since $x^2 + x + 1$ divides $x^3 - 1$, the corresponding linear action has order 3 in the mod-4 quotient, forcing the period-3 pattern in $S_n \bmod 4$.

Cyclotomic interpretation

The polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$ is the minimal polynomial of primitive cube roots of unity. When $k$ is odd, the recurrence behaves modulo 4 as if its eigenvalue were a cube root of unity on the unit circle $|z| = 1$ in $\mathbb{C}$.

10The Minkowski picture: the hyperbola orbit

Independently of congruences, conjugation places $\delta_k^n$ and $\delta_k^{-n}$ on a hyperbola. Set $x = \delta_k^n$, $y = \delta_k^{-n}$. Then $xy = 1$, so $(x,y)$ lies on the hyperbola $xy = 1$. Moreover,

$$S_n = \delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n} = x^2 + y^2, \qquad M_n(k) = \frac{S_n - 2}{4}.$$

Thus the "difference of consecutive square roots" condition is exactly the statement $x^2 + y^2 \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$, i.e. a congruence restriction on a natural coordinate of points on the hyperbola orbit.

In log-coordinates $u = \log x$, $v = \log y$, the hyperbola becomes the line $v = -u$ and the orbit is an arithmetic progression:

$$(u, v) = (n \log \delta_k,\; -n \log \delta_k).$$

The cyclotomic analysis explains why, when $k$ is odd, only every third step along this arithmetic progression lands in the congruence class that yields an integer $M_n(k)$.

The duality

These are the two faces of quadratic algebra:

Euclidean-Pythagorean: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ (circles, cyclotomic units, roots of unity)

Minkowskian-hyperbolic: $xy = c$ (hyperbolas, norm-one units, conjugate pairs)

The period-3 pattern arises from the cyclotomic viewpoint (mod-4 behaviour mimics cube roots of unity), while the consecutive-square-root representation is visible from the Minkowskian viewpoint (points on $xy = 1$).

11The even/odd dichotomy in quadratic rings

The even/odd dichotomy ultimately stems from the structure of rings of integers in quadratic fields. Let $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{D})$ where $D = k^2 + 4$.

When $k$ is even, $D \equiv 0 \pmod{4}$ and $\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$. Traces of algebraic integers lie in $\mathbb{Z}$, so $S_n = \operatorname{Tr}_{K/\mathbb{Q}}(\delta_k^{2n})$ is an integer with no denominator issues. Reducing modulo 4 gives a constant sequence.

When $k$ is odd, $D \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ and $\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}\!\left[\frac{1+\sqrt{D}}{2}\right]$. The shift to a "half-integer lattice" introduces a denominator-2 offset. While $S_n$ is still an integer (since $\delta_k^{2n}$ lies in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$, not the full $\mathcal{O}_K$), the finer structure of $\mathcal{O}_K$ causes the mod-4 projection to pick up the period-3 oscillation, reflecting the $x^2 + x + 1$ cyclotomic factor.

12Summary

For $\delta_k = (k + \sqrt{k^2+4})/2$, the reciprocal powers $\delta_k^{-n}$ admit a consecutive square-root representation

$$\delta_k^{-n} = \sqrt{M_n(k) + 1} - \sqrt{M_n(k)}$$

exactly when $S_n = \delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n} \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$, with $M_n(k) = (S_n - 2)/4$.

This holds for all $n$ when $k$ is even, and for $3 \mid n$ when $k$ is odd.

The parity phenomenon has three complementary explanations:

Finite-state: modulo 4, the recurrence becomes a finite update rule that must cycle. For odd $k$, the characteristic polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$ forces period 3.

Cyclotomic: the polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$ is the minimal polynomial of primitive cube roots of unity. The mod-4 behaviour mimics roots of unity on the circle $|z| = 1$.

Minkowskian: points $(\delta_k^n, \delta_k^{-n})$ lie on the hyperbola $xy = 1$. The integrality condition $S_n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ is a congruence on the coordinate $x^2 + y^2$ along this orbit.

The even/odd dichotomy stems from the structure of quadratic rings: when $k$ is odd, the ring of integers $\mathbb{Z}[(1 + \sqrt{D})/2]$ has a half-integer lattice that introduces the period-3 oscillation.
