Working Paper · Algebraic Number Theory

Metallic Means and Differences of Consecutive Square Roots: a Cyclotomic Obstruction

Lee McCulloch-James
Draft, 2026
Abstract

The metallic mean $\delta_k = (k+\sqrt{k^2+4})/2$ generalises the golden and silver ratios. Every positive integer power of its reciprocal is a candidate for the representation $\delta_k^{-n} = \sqrt{M+1}-\sqrt{M}$ with $M$ a non-negative integer. For even $k$ the representation holds at every exponent. For odd $k$ it succeeds only when the exponent is divisible by 3.

The generator formula and the underlying Lucas-type recurrence are standard. What is not obvious is why the obstruction has period exactly 3. The recurrence modulo 4 acts on a state space of 16 elements; the period could be anything from 1 to 16. We show that the answer is cyclotomic. The characteristic polynomial of the recurrence is $x^2-(k^2+2)x+1$. For odd $k$, the coefficient $-(k^2+2)$ reduces to $+1$ modulo 4 (since $-3 \equiv 1$), and the polynomial becomes $x^2+x+1 = \Phi_3(x)$, the third cyclotomic polynomial. The roots of $\Phi_3$ have multiplicative order 3, forcing the period-3 oscillation. For even $k$ the polynomial degenerates to $(x-1)^2$ and no obstruction arises.

Over $\mathbb{Z}$ the characteristic polynomial has real irrational roots with no connection to roots of unity, but the mod-4 projection causes it to coincide with $\Phi_3$. The even/odd dichotomy traces to the discriminant $D = k^2+4$, which determines whether the quadratic integer ring $\mathcal{O}_K$ is $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$ (even $k$, $D \equiv 0 \pmod{4}$) or the larger half-integer lattice $\mathbb{Z}[(1+\sqrt{D})/2]$ (odd $k$, $D \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$). Geometrically, three binary quadratic forms govern the problem: the Minkowskian form $XY$ (the orbit), the Euclidean form $X^2+Y^2$ (the integrality test), and the Eisenstein form $X^2+XY+Y^2$ (the obstruction). The period-3 pattern is the statement that, modulo 4, the Eisenstein form mediates between the other two.

Notation and conventions
$k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$: the metallic-mean parameter
$\delta_k = (k + \sqrt{k^2 + 4})/2$: the metallic mean (positive root of $x^2 - kx - 1 = 0$)
$S_n(k) = \delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n}$: a Lucas-type trace sequence
$M_n(k) = (S_n - 2)/4$: the generator candidate
$\Phi_3(x) = x^2 + x + 1$: the third cyclotomic polynomial
$D = k^2 + 4$: discriminant; $\mathcal{O}_K$: ring of integers of $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{D})$

1The observation and the question it raises

The silver ratio is $\delta_2 = 1 + \sqrt{2}$, the positive root of $x^2 - 2x - 1 = 0$. Its reciprocal is $\delta_2^{-1} = \sqrt{2} - 1$ (rationalise $1/(1+\sqrt{2})$ to check). Successive powers of this reciprocal produce:

$$(\sqrt{2}-1)^2 = \sqrt{9} - \sqrt{8}, \qquad (\sqrt{2}-1)^3 = \sqrt{50} - \sqrt{49}, \qquad (\sqrt{2}-1)^4 = \sqrt{289} - \sqrt{288}.$$

Every power is the difference of square roots of consecutive integers. The integers under the roots form the sequence $\{0, 1, 8, 49, 288, 1681, \ldots\}$, which is OEIS A001108. This was observed in a McMurmerings blog post [1], where a generating function was derived by brute-force binomial expansion.

Now generalise. The metallic mean with parameter $k$ is $\delta_k = (k + \sqrt{k^2+4})/2$. For which $k$ and $n$ does $\delta_k^{-n} = \sqrt{M+1} - \sqrt{M}$ hold for some non-negative integer $M$?

The answer splits sharply by parity of $k$. For even $k$ the representation holds for every $n$. For odd $k$ it holds only when $3 \mid n$. The generator formula and the Lucas recurrence that produce $M$ are standard. What is not obvious is the period-3 obstruction for odd $k$. That is the subject of this paper.

2Setup: metallic means, the generator formula, the recurrence

Fix $k \geq 1$. The metallic mean $\delta_k$ is the positive root of $x^2 - kx - 1 = 0$:

$$\delta_k = \frac{k + \sqrt{k^2+4}}{2}, \qquad \delta_k > 1, \qquad 0 < \delta_k^{-1} < 1.$$ (1)

A number $y \in (0,1)$ can be written as $y = \sqrt{M+1} - \sqrt{M}$ for $M \geq 0$ if and only if $M = (y^{-2} + y^2 - 2)/4$. (Rationalise: $1/y = \sqrt{M+1} + \sqrt{M}$; square the difference.) Setting $y = \delta_k^{-n}$:

$$M_n(k) = \frac{\delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n} - 2}{4} = \frac{S_n(k) - 2}{4},$$ (2)

where $S_n(k) = \delta_k^{2n} + \delta_k^{-2n}$. Since $\delta_k^2 + \delta_k^{-2} = k^2 + 2$, the sequence $S_n$ satisfies

Lucas-type recurrence
$$S_{n+1} = (k^2+2)\,S_n - S_{n-1}, \qquad S_0 = 2,\; S_1 = k^2+2.$$ (3)

The coefficients and initial values are integers, so $S_n \in \mathbb{Z}$ for all $n$. The representation question reduces to:

$$M_n(k) \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \iff S_n(k) \equiv 2 \pmod{4}.$$ (4)

This is where the interesting structure begins.

3The parity theorem

Theorem 1

1. If $k$ is even, then $S_n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ for all $n \geq 0$. Every exponent works.

2. If $k$ is odd, then $S_n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ if and only if $3 \mid n$. Only every third exponent works.

Proof sketch. Write $A = k^2 + 2$. For even $k$, $A \equiv 2$ mod 4 and induction on $S_{n+1} \equiv 2S_n - S_{n-1}$ gives $S_n \equiv 2$ for all $n$. For odd $k$, $A \equiv 3$ mod 4 and the recurrence mod 4 becomes $S_{n+1} \equiv -S_n - S_{n-1}$, producing the cycle $2, 3, 3, 2, 3, 3, \ldots$ with period 3. $\square$

The proof is elementary. The question it raises is not.

4The central question: why period 3?

A linear recurrence modulo a finite ring must be eventually periodic. That it cycles is guaranteed. That it cycles with period exactly 3 is not. The state space $(\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z})^2$ has 16 elements, so the period could be anything from 1 to 16. Why does the odd-$k$ recurrence select period 3?

Put differently: what algebraic structure forces the number 3 to appear in a problem that, on its face, involves only the number 4 (the modulus) and the number 2 (the parity of $k$)?

The answer lies in the characteristic polynomial of the recurrence, and specifically in the cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_3(x) = x^2 + x + 1$.

5The characteristic polynomial modulo 4

The recurrence (3) has characteristic polynomial

$$f(x) = x^2 - (k^2+2)x + 1.$$ (5)

Its roots over $\mathbb{R}$ are $\lambda_k = \delta_k^2$ and $\lambda_k^{-1} = \delta_k^{-2}$, with $\lambda_k \lambda_k^{-1} = 1$ (the constant term). Reduce modulo 4. Since $k^2 \bmod 4$ depends only on the parity of $k$:

Characteristic polynomial mod 4
$$f(x) \equiv \begin{cases} x^2 - 2x + 1 = (x-1)^2, & k \text{ even},\\ x^2 + x + 1, & k \text{ odd}. \end{cases} \pmod{4}$$ (6)

For even $k$, the polynomial has a double root at $x = 1$. For odd $k$, it becomes $x^2 + x + 1$. This is the third cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_3(x)$.

6The cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_3$ and its arithmetic

The cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_n(x)$ is the minimal polynomial of primitive $n$-th roots of unity over $\mathbb{Q}$. For $n = 3$:

$$\Phi_3(x) = x^2 + x + 1 = \frac{x^3 - 1}{x - 1}.$$ (7)

Its roots over $\mathbb{C}$ are $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ and $\bar{\omega} = e^{-2\pi i/3}$, the primitive cube roots of unity. These satisfy $\omega^3 = 1$ and $\omega \neq 1$.

Over $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$, the polynomial $\Phi_3(x) = x^2 + x + 1$ is irreducible (neither $x = 0, 1, 2, 3$ is a root mod 4). The quotient ring $(\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z})[x]/\Phi_3(x)$ is the ring in which the recurrence eigenvalues live when reduced mod 4. The multiplicative order of $x$ in this quotient divides 3 (since $x^3 \equiv 1$), and since $\Phi_3$ is irreducible the order is exactly 3.

Key mechanism

The eigenvalues of the recurrence, reduced modulo 4, are roots of $\Phi_3$. Their multiplicative order is 3. Any sequence governed by these eigenvalues inherits period 3 modulo 4. The number 3 enters the problem through the factorisation of $x^3 - 1$, not through any property of the metallic means themselves.

This is the sense in which the obstruction is cyclotomic. The period-3 pattern is not a coincidence of the initial values or the recurrence coefficient. It is forced by the algebraic structure of the characteristic polynomial over $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.

7From $\Phi_3$ to the period-3 obstruction

The general term of the recurrence can be written $S_n = c_1 \lambda^n + c_2 \lambda^{-n}$ where $\lambda$ is a root of $f(x)$. Modulo 4, when $k$ is odd, $\lambda$ satisfies $\lambda^2 + \lambda + 1 \equiv 0$. Therefore $\lambda^3 \equiv 1$ and the sequence $S_n \bmod 4$ has period dividing 3.

The initial values determine the phase: $S_0 \equiv 2$, $S_1 \equiv 3$, $S_2 \equiv 3$, $S_3 \equiv 2$, and the cycle repeats. The condition $S_n \equiv 2$ holds exactly at $n \equiv 0 \pmod{3}$.

The obstruction to the consecutive-square-root representation is therefore: for odd $k$, the eigenvalue of the recurrence is a cube root of unity modulo 4, and only every third power of a cube root returns to 1. The exponents where the representation works are exactly those divisible by 3.

Remark: why not period 2, 5, 7, ...?

The period is controlled by the factorisation of $f(x)$ modulo 4. Since $k^2 + 2 \equiv 3$ for all odd $k$, the reduced polynomial is always $\Phi_3$, regardless of which odd $k$ we choose. The period is locked at 3 by the cyclotomic structure. A different modulus (say, reducing mod 3 or mod 5) would give different periods, but the integrality condition for $M_n$ specifically requires reduction mod 4.

8The even case: degeneration to $(x-1)^2$

For even $k$, the characteristic polynomial mod 4 is $(x-1)^2$. The eigenvalue $\lambda \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, so $\lambda^n \equiv 1$ for all $n$, and $S_n \equiv 2$ for all $n$. There is no periodicity because there is no obstruction: the eigenvalue is already 1.

Algebraically: $(x-1)^2$ divides $x^1 - 1$ in the sense that its root has multiplicative order 1. The "period" is 1, which means every exponent works.

The contrast is clean. Even $k$ gives $(x-1)^2$: order 1, no obstruction. Odd $k$ gives $\Phi_3 = x^2 + x + 1$: order 3, period-3 obstruction.

9The quadratic ring perspective

The even/odd dichotomy has a deeper source in the arithmetic of quadratic fields. Let $D = k^2 + 4$ and $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{D})$.

When $k$ is even, $D \equiv 0 \pmod{4}$. The ring of integers is $\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$, and the metallic mean $\delta_k$ lies in $\mathcal{O}_K$. The trace $S_n = \operatorname{Tr}_{K/\mathbb{Q}}(\delta_k^{2n})$ lands in $\mathbb{Z}$ with no denominator complications. The characteristic polynomial of the Frobenius-like map on the trace sequence factors as $(x-1)^2$ mod 4, reflecting the fact that the lattice $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$ is "aligned" with the mod-4 reduction.

When $k$ is odd, $D \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$. Now $\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[(1+\sqrt{D})/2]$, a strictly larger ring than $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$. The metallic mean $\delta_k = (k + \sqrt{D})/2$ lies in $\mathcal{O}_K$ but not in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$. The "half-integer lattice" of $\mathcal{O}_K$ introduces a misalignment with the mod-4 reduction. The trace $S_n$ is still an integer (since $\delta_k^{2n} \in \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$), but its residue mod 4 now oscillates.

The oscillation period is 3 because the characteristic polynomial of the trace recurrence, reduced mod 4, is the cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_3$. The half-integer lattice structure of $\mathcal{O}_K$ is what causes the characteristic polynomial to reduce to $\Phi_3$ rather than to $(x-1)^2$.

The chain of causation

$k$ odd $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $D \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $\mathcal{O}_K$ is a half-integer lattice $\;\Rightarrow\;$ characteristic polynomial $\equiv \Phi_3 \pmod{4}$ $\;\Rightarrow\;$ eigenvalue has order 3 $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $S_n \bmod 4$ has period 3 $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $M_n \in \mathbb{Z}$ only when $3 \mid n$.

10The Minkowski picture

There is a geometric face to this story. Set $x = \delta_k^n$, $y = \delta_k^{-n}$. Then $xy = 1$ and $(x,y)$ lies on the hyperbola $xy = 1$. The trace is $S_n = x^2 + y^2$, so the integrality condition $S_n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ is a congruence on the Euclidean-Pythagorean coordinate $x^2 + y^2$ evaluated at points on the Minkowskian-hyperbolic curve $xy = 1$.

In log-coordinates $(u, v) = (n \log \delta_k, -n \log \delta_k)$, the orbit is an arithmetic progression on the line $v = -u$. The cyclotomic obstruction says that for odd $k$, only every third point of this arithmetic progression satisfies the Euclidean congruence.

Two faces of quadratic algebra

Euclidean-Pythagorean: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ (circles, cyclotomic units, roots of unity). This is where the period-3 pattern lives.

Minkowskian-hyperbolic: $xy = c$ (hyperbolas, norm-one units, conjugate pairs). This is where the metallic-mean orbit lives.

The consecutive-square-root condition sits at the intersection: it asks for an arithmetic property ($S_n \equiv 2 \bmod 4$) of the Euclidean coordinate ($x^2 + y^2$) along the Minkowskian orbit ($xy = 1$).

11Examples

11.1Golden ($k = 1$, odd)

$\delta_1 = \varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$. The representation holds for $3 \mid n$: $\varphi^{-3} = \sqrt{5} - \sqrt{4}$ ($M_3 = 4$); $\varphi^{-6} = \sqrt{81} - \sqrt{80}$ ($M_6 = 80$). At $n = 1, 2, 4, 5, \ldots$ the generator $M_n$ is not an integer.

11.2Silver ($k = 2$, even)

$\delta_2 = 1 + \sqrt{2}$. The representation holds for all $n$. The generator sequence $\{M_n\} = \{0, 1, 8, 49, 288, 1681, 9800, \ldots\}$ is OEIS A001108, with recurrence $M_{n+1} = 6M_n - M_{n-1} + 2$.

11.3Bronze ($k = 3$, odd)

$\delta_3 = (3+\sqrt{13})/2$. Again only $3 \mid n$ works: $\delta_3^{-3} = \sqrt{325} - \sqrt{324}$ ($M_3 = 324$); $\delta_3^{-6} = \sqrt{421201} - \sqrt{421200}$ ($M_6 = 421200$).

11.4The generator recurrence

Since $S_n = 4M_n + 2$, the recurrence for $M_n$ is $M_{n+1} = (k^2+2)M_n - M_{n-1} + k^2/2$. This is an integer recurrence when $k$ is even. For odd $k$, restrict to the subsequence $n = 3m$.

12Discussion

The generator formula and the Lucas recurrence for $S_n$ are standard consequences of quadratic algebra. The content of this paper is the explanation of why the period-3 obstruction appears for odd metallic-mean parameters.

The answer is that the characteristic polynomial of the recurrence, reduced modulo 4, becomes the third cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_3(x) = x^2 + x + 1$. This polynomial is the minimal polynomial of primitive cube roots of unity. Its roots have multiplicative order exactly 3. Any sequence whose evolution is governed by eigenvalues satisfying $\Phi_3$ must have period 3 modulo 4. The integrality condition $S_n \equiv 2$ therefore holds at every third step and fails elsewhere.

The even/odd dichotomy is not accidental. It traces through the discriminant $D = k^2 + 4$ to the structure of the quadratic integer ring $\mathcal{O}_K$: when $D \equiv 0$ mod 4, the ring is $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$ and the characteristic polynomial degenerates; when $D \equiv 1$ mod 4, the ring acquires a half-integer lattice and the characteristic polynomial becomes cyclotomic.

Three things are therefore not independent: the parity of $k$, the lattice structure of the ring of integers, and the appearance of $\Phi_3$ in the mod-4 reduction. The period-3 obstruction is where they meet.

12.1Three quadratic forms

The paper involves three binary quadratic forms, each with a geometric identity:

Minkowskian: $XY = Z^2$ (hyperbola, norm-one units, the orbit).

Euclidean: $X^2 + Y^2$ (circle, Gaussian integers, the trace $S_n$).

Eisenstein: $X^2 + XY + Y^2$ (hexagon, Eisenstein integers, $\Phi_3$).

The orbit of a metallic mean lies on the first. The integrality test ($S_n \equiv 2 \bmod 4$) is phrased in terms of the second. But the periodicity of that test is controlled by the third.

In projective coordinates $[X:Y:Z]$ with $x = X/Z$, $y = Y/Z$, the hyperbola $xy = 1$ becomes the conic $XY = Z^2$ and the trace becomes $S_n = (X^2 + Y^2)/Z^2$. The condition $S_n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}$ is then $X^2 + Y^2 \equiv 2Z^2 \pmod{4}$: a congruence involving the Euclidean form evaluated on the Minkowskian conic. Reducing the characteristic polynomial of the recurrence modulo 4 produces $\Phi_3(x) = x^2 + x + 1$, whose associated quadratic form is the Eisenstein norm $a^2 + ab + b^2$. It is this third form that determines which points on the Minkowskian conic satisfy the Euclidean congruence.

The three forms correspond to the three rank-2 integral binary quadratic forms of small discriminant: $\Delta = -4$ (Euclidean, lattice $\mathbb{Z}[i]$), $\Delta = -3$ (Eisenstein, lattice $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$), and $\Delta > 0$ (Minkowskian, lattice $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{D}]$ or its half-integer extension). That the period-3 obstruction arises from $\Phi_3$ is, in this language, the statement that the Eisenstein form mediates between the Minkowskian orbit and the Euclidean test when both are projected into $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.
